Article 01

A Neuroscience Perspective on Sensory Sensitivity

People often assume that sensitivity to everyday environments is psychological or exaggerated. From a neuroscience point of view, that explanation doesn't really hold up.

What makes more sense is neuronal hyperexcitability leading to central sensitization. When neurons become hyperexcitable, they start firing too easily. The balance between excitation and inhibition breaks down, often due to ion channel dysfunction, neurotransmitter imbalance, or sustained neuroinflammatory signaling. The nervous system becomes reactive instead of selective.

"This isn't a fringe idea. Similar mechanisms are already well established in epilepsy, migraine, and neuropathic pain."

If this hyperexcitable state persists, the brain's sensory processing changes more broadly. This is where central sensitization comes in. The nervous system amplifies incoming signals and loses its ability to filter out harmless stimuli.

At that point, the brain doesn't just register a smell, sound, or sensation. It interprets it as a threat. That's why low levels of common chemicals — fragrances, cleaning agents, vehicle exhaust, or new materials — can trigger real, disabling symptoms in some individuals. The response isn't imagined and it isn't voluntary. It's the nervous system misclassifying normal input as danger.

Symptoms like headaches, brain fog, nausea, fatigue, and muscle pain are not abstract experiences. They reflect altered neural processing and sustained activation of stress and defense pathways.

Looking at sensitivity through this lens matters. It moves the conversation away from dismissal and toward biology. It also highlights the need for better diagnostic tools that integrate sensory processing, symptom burden, and neural excitability rather than focusing on exposure levels alone.

Sensitivity, in this context, isn't weakness. It's a nervous system stuck in a high-gain state. Understanding that is essential — for research, for clinical practice, and for how we treat people experiencing these conditions.

Core Mechanisms

Three Shifts That Change Everything

⚡

Ion Channel Dysfunction

When voltage-gated channels fail to regulate ionic flow correctly, action potential thresholds drop. Neurons fire with less provocation, progressively lowering the barrier to activation.

⚖️

E/I Imbalance

The normal interplay between excitatory (glutamate) and inhibitory (GABA) neurotransmitters becomes skewed. Without adequate inhibitory damping, excitation propagates too freely.

🔥

Neuroinflammatory Signaling

Sustained cytokine and glial activity maintains a chronic inflammatory state in neural tissue. This directly lowers thresholds and prolongs activation windows across sensory circuits.

Article 02

When Sensory Systems
Lose Their Brakes

Two mechanisms sit at the center of sensory sensitivity:

  • Excitation–inhibition imbalance
  • Persistent stress signaling

Under normal conditions, sensory input is selectively gated. Excitatory responses are constrained by inhibitory circuits, and most stimuli never escalate beyond background noise. When inhibition weakens, excitation loses precision. Neurons fire too easily, too broadly, and remain active longer than they should.

At the same time, sustained stress signaling biases the system toward threat detection. Stress pathways don't just respond to stimuli — they recalibrate thresholds, amplify responses, and prolong activation. Together, these shifts place the nervous system in a high-gain state. Input is amplified, filtering degrades, and harmless stimuli acquire salience.

"Once regulation is lost, the environment doesn't need to be extreme to feel overwhelming."

Another piece that often gets overlooked is Hebbian plasticity. In a system already biased toward hyperexcitability, sensory input and stress signaling co-activate repeatedly. When this happens, the underlying circuits strengthen — not because the stimulus is inherently harmful, but because the system is firing together in a defensive state.

Over time, responses become easier to trigger and harder to shut down. Thresholds drop, generalization increases, and the nervous system begins to anticipate rather than react.

Once these patterns are reinforced, removing the original trigger doesn't automatically reverse them. The network has adapted to operate in a state of heightened alert. That's why persistence matters as much as initiation when we talk about sensory sensitivity.

And why plasticity, in this context, becomes part of the problem — not the solution.

Clinical Implication

Removing the trigger alone is insufficient. The reinforced network persists and continues to generate responses in the absence of the original stimulus.

Takeaway

Biology, Not Dismissal

Understanding sensory sensitivity through the lens of neural excitability and plasticity shifts the conversation from psychological dismissal toward mechanism-based medicine. Better diagnostic tools must integrate sensory processing, symptom burden, and neural excitability — not exposure levels alone.
